The history of the symbolic wedding osariya

May 02, 2018 12:56 pm

“The traditional bridal osariya is the most venerated and unique costume for the Sinhalese woman. She looks and feels nothing short of royalty on her big day.

In conversation with Anoma Athukorale, the Presidential Award-winning beautician and therapist from the reputed Anoma and Angeeka Athukorala Salons and Academy.

The traditional osariya which derive from the word ‘ohoriya’ comes to this beautiful pearl island from the southern part of India back in the days when the Malabar princesses married the last Nayakkar princes of Kandy.

The Radala women who belonged to a higher society in Kandy were the first local people to wear the osariya or ‘Kandyan Saree’ from there on.

The saree would be worn in different styles of cotton, batik, handloom and silk, hand painted in geometric and floral designs and even religious motifs.

This modest garment would evolve to be the national dress of the Sinhalese women of Sri Lanka and is worn all over the world as well.

What makes the osariya distinct from the other sarees is the elaborate frill at the side of the waist called the odokkuwa and the frilled border that falls off the shoulder called the 'osari pota'.

A traditional Osari would have the frills turned the right way around in contrast to the everyday osariya. The total length of the osariya could vary between five to six yards of material.

A Kandyan osari clad woman was a symbol of the country’s rich heritage and sinhala buddhist culture. The traditional wedding osariya consists of a jacket with a round neckline that covers the chest, midriff and back with preferred puffed up sleeves and is covered by an unstitched cloth that is approximately 15 feet long draped around the lower body and a shoulder drape (or fall of the saree) called pota which falls to the back.

The drape was inspired by the uthurusaluwa a piece of cloth thrown over the shoulder by women in that era when they would go on outings.

It also had an ending that was triangular shaped called ul pota-ohoriya which is still worn by the older generation today.

Colours of the outfit vary from different hues of white with heavy embroidery and mostly of silk material.

White was the standard colour used as a symbol of the bride’s chastity and purity. Different variations of white is used depending on the bride’s caste.

The bride would also wear 26 pieces of jewellery from head to waist. These range from what is known as the Konda Kuura, Agasthi to the Hawadiya. The jewellery is handmade which reflected the Kandyan nobility, royalty and their prosperity. 

Weddings are an important event in one’s life and the variation of the Kandyan Osariya had gradually evolved with slight changes depending on the region and caste of the bride.

The upcountry Govigama caste would wear one kind and the lower, less economically inclined caste would have another. With the Dutch invasion most of the southern brides were seen to be adorned with a veil to complete their attire.

"Most of the brides that come to us are unaware of the cultural values and aspects of the osariya so we take pride in advising them to make smart choices with their attire, balancing modern fashion with traditional elements still intact.” 

- Anoma Athukorale

“The beeralu lace design used to be quite famous on the traditional osariya of a Sri Lankan bride. It is sad to see that it is a dying tradition which is more sought after by foreigners than the locals”, 

- Angeeka Athukorala de Silva daughter of Mrs. Anoma Athukorale.

In the ever-changing landscape of bridal fashion in Sri Lanka, the symbolic wedding osariya has evolved from variations of colour, style and length. 

Therefore while keeping the essence and purpose of the sacred dress intact let us preserve the iconic osari inspiring the future Sinhalese woman for generations to come!
