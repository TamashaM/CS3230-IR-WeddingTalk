Making An Interfaith Marriage Work

July 12, 2017 3:15 pm

More and more of us are heading in to interfaith marriages. The bringing together of two religions and two cultural backgrounds in to a home is a beautiful thing but one that needs to be managed with care. Here are some thoughts on how to set about it.

No Attempts At Saving Your Spouse’s Soul 

When you love someone it is natural that you wish the best for them. No matter how strongly you believe in a religious teaching or tenant, do not be tempted to preach or brainwash your spouse in to believing the same. You might think it is your moral duty to do so, but what it does is violate a fundamental rule in marriage; that of mutual respect.

If you have chosen to marry someone outside your faith, you have also chosen to accept that person for who they are. Which means you have to accept that you just may not see eye to eye on certain religious beliefs. 

Genuinely Celebrate Each Others Religious Festivals

Religious festivals are a wonderful opportunity show how much you respect your spouse’s religious beliefs. Don’t shy away from celebrating these occasions together and be genuine in your efforts when doing so. Learn a bit about traditions and customs which may be rather foreign to you.

Keep in mind that Christmas, Vesak, Ramadan, Sinhala and Hindu Avurudu celebrations are so much more than just mere religious festivals. These are also cultural celebrations which bring family and friends together. They will only add color to your lives and present opportunities to create beautiful memories. 

Religion can be a Sensitive Topic 

Religion plays differing roles in different peoples’ lives. Some are devout practitioners and overtly religious and some are very discreet in their religious practices and beliefs. So whether you make a show of it or not, the point is that religion is a very personal matter.

Try not to expect your spouse to subscribe to the exact manner in which you have chosen to practice your faith. While you do still share many things in common, it is more advisable that you simply let religion continue to be within the personal domain. Don’t judge. 

What About The Children? 

The biggest challenge in an interfaith marriage is how to bridge the religious and cultural gap when raising children. Couples take many routes when dealing with this situation. Some resort to providing a multi religious atmosphere for their children and some decide that the children take their mother or father’s religion entirely.

Whatever the approach is it is prudent to discuss openly regarding this matter prior to marriage. It is a decision you and your spouse will most likely feel very strongly about and this can present a problem if you don’t see eye to eye regarding it after marriage. 

Empath and more Empathy

Practicing two different faiths under one roof is a tricky business, and you are most likely to offend your partner once in a way. The ability to put your self in your partner’s shoes (also known as empathy) on a regular basis will help you navigate these difficult situations wisely.

Empathetically understanding that your partners religious views are important to him or her the same way that yours are important to you. Never let emotions run high should there ever be a small misunderstanding. Expect these to occur and therefore be prepared to handle them.  

Keeping The Uninvited Religious Crusaders At Bay 

Much to your surprise you may find that in an interfaith marriage the greatest challenges directed your way will come from in-laws, well meaning extended family, religious clergy, friends or sometimes even complete strangers. There are many who still strongly believe and wish that marriage should be between a man and woman of one faith and may not fully accept anything less.

Sometimes such persons will take it upon themselves to lecture your spouse on religious beliefs, criticize religious practices and even try to convert your spouse. Protecting each other from such persons should be your priority. Let everyone know that you have accepted your spouses for who he or she is and that the agreements between you two are non-negotiable.

Be Strong In Your Own Faith

Don’t compromise or neglect practicing your own faith to the extent you need to, for the fear of alienating your spouse. It is sometimes boring to go to church alone on Sunday or to visit a temple on poya day all by yourself. You might consider opting out of certain religious observances as you feel your spouse is either not too keen or is uncomfortable participating in.

While this is a very considerate gesture on your part it might not be the best move in the long run, especially if you are someone who is truly guided by your faith. If it is important for you to devote time for your spiritual wellbeing, let your husband or wife know that it is so. Work out a feasible way in which you can meet this need.
